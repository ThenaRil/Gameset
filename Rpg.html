RPG Genre

History.

The role-playing video game genre began in the mid-1970s on mainframe computers, inspired by pen-and-paper role-playing games such as Dungeons & Dragons. Several other sources of inspiration for early role-playing video games also included tabletop wargames, sports simulation games, adventure games such as Colossal Cave Adventure, and fantasy writings by authors such as J. R. R. Tolkien, traditional strategy games such as chess, and ancient epic literature dating back to the Epic of Gilgamesh, which followed the same basic structure of setting off in various quests in order to accomplish goals.

After the success of role-playing video games such as Ultima and Wizardry, which in turn served as the blueprint for Dragon Quest and Final Fantasy, the role-playing genre eventually diverged into two styles, Eastern role-playing games and Western role-playing games, due to cultural differences, though roughly mirroring the platform divide between consoles and computers, respectively. Finally, while the first RPGs offered strictly a single player experience, the popularity of multiplayer modes rose sharply during the early to mid-1990s with action role-playing games such as Secret of Mana and Diablo. With the advent of the Internet, multiplayer games have grown to become massively multiplayer online role-playing games (MMORPG), including Lineage, Final Fantasy XI, and World of Warcraft.

Infographics.

RPG video games have evolved dramatically since their origins in tabletop games like Dungeons & Dragons. Early computer RPGs in the 1980s, such as Ultima and Wizardry, adapted tabletop mechanics for digital platforms. The 1990s saw JRPGs like Final Fantasy and Chrono Trigger dominate consoles, blending narrative depth with character customization. Western RPGs like Baldur's Gate in the late 1990s introduced expansive worlds and player choice.

The 2000s brought 3D open-world RPGs like The Elder Scrolls and Mass Effect, combining storytelling with player agency. Modern RPGs, including The Witcher 3 and Elden Ring, emphasize expansive worlds, cinematic storytelling, and freedom of playstyle, solidifying the genre's mainstream appeal.

Gameplay Mechanics.

Zenless Zone Zero features dynamic and engaging gameplay mechanics set in a futuristic, post-apocalyptic world. Players control teams of three characters (Agents), each with unique skills and elemental attributes such as Fire, Ice, and Electric. The combat system emphasizes fast-paced, real-time action with mechanics like timed dodges, chain attacks, and ultimate abilities. Players can switch characters mid-combat to maximize damage and exploit synergies. The game includes a variety of modes, including a story-driven campaign and a roguelike exploration mode called "Hollows," where players navigate procedurally generated dungeons. Players also engage in side missions, arcade mini-games, and special challenges for rewards and character upgrades. Central to gameplay is managing resources like the Decibel Gauge, which powers ultimate attacks, and equipping Agents with gear to enhance their abilities
